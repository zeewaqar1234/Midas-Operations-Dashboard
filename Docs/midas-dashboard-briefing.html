Section 1

What is Midas?

Midas is a Berlin-based fintech that takes traditional financial products — specifically US Treasury bills and a crypto basis trade strategy — and makes them accessible on the Ethereum blockchain as standard ERC-20 tokens.

Think of it this way: instead of a fund manager giving you a PDF statement that says "you own $50,000 of T-bills," Midas gives you 50,000 digital tokens in your crypto wallet that represent exactly that claim. Each token is always redeemable for its current USD value.

The core idea

Tokenization = taking a real-world financial asset and representing ownership of it as an on-chain token. Midas issues these tokens (mTokens), manages the underlying assets off-chain, and runs the on-chain infrastructure (smart contracts, oracle, custody) that connects the two worlds.

The dashboard was built as an internal operations tool — not something investors see. Its job is to let the operations team check the protocol's health in real time: Is the price feed current? Are investors subscribing or redeeming? Who has permission to mint new tokens?

Section 2

The Two Products

mTBILL — Tokenized T-Bill Exposure

mTBILL gives holders the economic return of holding short-duration US Treasury bills. The underlying assets are actual T-bills held in a segregated account (custodied via a Luxembourg SPV structure).

The token's NAV (Net Asset Value) starts at $1.00 on day one and slowly climbs over time as the T-bills accrue interest. If the current T-bill yield is roughly 5% per year, then after one year the NAV is approximately $1.05. This is how the holder earns a return — not through dividends or coupon payments, but through the rising price of the token itself.

Simple analogy

mTBILL is like a money market fund unit that you can send to anyone on Ethereum in the same transaction as any other token. The price goes up daily, reflecting the yield of the underlying T-bills.

mBASIS — Tokenized Basis Trade

A basis trade in crypto means: go long on spot Bitcoin or Ethereum (buy the actual asset), and simultaneously open a short position on the equivalent futures contract. The profit comes from the difference in price between the spot market and the futures market — this difference is called the basis.

In most market conditions, crypto futures trade at a premium to spot (because traders are willing to pay more for future delivery). This premium, collected repeatedly, generates the yield for mBASIS holders.

Key difference from mTBILL

mTBILL has an on-chain oracle that publishes its price daily. mBASIS has no public oracle — its NAV is reported directly by the strategy manager. This is why the dashboard shows "Via strategy manager" for mBASIS pricing, not a live feed.

Section 3

How the NAV Price Works

Every time an investor subscribes to mTBILL or redeems it, the protocol needs a fair, tamper-proof price to use. That price is stored on the Ethereum blockchain through a system called an oracle.

The Midas oracle follows the Chainlink price feed standard. Chainlink is the industry-standard system for pushing off-chain data (like prices) onto a blockchain in a verifiable way. Midas uses the same interface but operates its own oracle, controlled through its MPC workspace.

The staleness check

Once the oracle is updated, the timestamp of the last update is stored on-chain. The dashboard checks this timestamp on every load. If more than 24 hours have passed without an update, the dashboard raises a risk flag and the instant subscription/redemption mode should be paused — because using a stale price could be unfair to investors.

AUM — Total Assets Under Management

At any given moment, the Total AUM of a product is simply:

Formula 1 — Assets Under Management
$$\text{AUM} = \text{Total Supply} \times \text{NAV Price}$$

For example: if there are 10,000,000 mTBILL tokens in circulation, and the current NAV price is $1.0487, then the AUM is $10,487,000. Every time the strategy manager pushes a new NAV, this number updates automatically across the whole protocol.

Section 4

Subscriptions and Redemptions

Because mTokens represent real financial products, you cannot simply buy them on a crypto exchange. Investors must go through a formal process — just like subscribing to a fund — to ensure KYC compliance and correct pricing.

Subscription (Mint)

When an investor wants to subscribe, they send USDC (a USD-pegged stablecoin) to the Midas deposit address. In return, the operations team mints the corresponding amount of mTokens at the current NAV price.

Two Settlement Modes

  • Instant mode: if the MSL liquidity pool has enough USDC (typically up to 10% of TVL), the investor gets tokens in the same blockchain transaction. Settlement is near-instant.
  • Standard mode: the request is queued, the strategy manager deploys capital into the underlying assets, and tokens are minted within T+2 to T+7 business days. This is equivalent to fund standard settlement.

Redemption (Burn)

To redeem, the investor sends their mTokens back to the protocol. The operations team burns (destroys) the tokens and sends USDC back to the investor's wallet at the current NAV price.

Why "mint" and "burn"?

In blockchain terminology, minting means creating new tokens, and burning means permanently destroying them. Both events are permanently recorded on-chain and are visible to anyone on Etherscan. The dashboard reads these events to track all capital flows in real time.

The MPC Workspace

Neither subscriptions nor redemptions happen automatically. Every mint and burn transaction must be signed through the MPC (Multi-Party Computation) workspace — typically Fordefi. This means:

  • The strategy manager can propose a transaction
  • Midas must co-sign it before it broadcasts to the network
  • This is the "2-of-N quorum" — two parties must approve every action

This design ensures no single person — not even the strategy manager — can unilaterally mint tokens or move funds.

Section 5

The Key Metrics — Where Every Number Comes From

Every number shown on the dashboard is either read directly from the Ethereum blockchain or calculated from on-chain data. Here is exactly where each metric originates and how it is computed.

Implied Annual Yield (mTBILL only)

Since the NAV price of mTBILL rises continuously as T-bills accrue interest, we can estimate the annualized yield by comparing today's price to the price 30 days ago and projecting that rate forward.

Formula 2 — Implied Annual Yield (30-day annualisation)
$$\text{Implied APY} = \left(\frac{P_{\text{now}}}{P_{\text{30d ago}}}\right)^{\!\tfrac{365}{30}} - 1$$

Where \( P_{\text{now}} \) is today's oracle price and \( P_{\text{30d ago}} \) is the closest historical oracle reading to 30 days ago. The exponent \( \frac{365}{30} \) converts a 30-day return into an annual rate.

Example: if \( P_{\text{now}} = 1.0487 \) and \( P_{\text{30d ago}} = 1.0447 \), then the 30-day return is \(\frac{1.0487}{1.0447} - 1 \approx 0.383\%\), and the implied APY is approximately \( 4.77\% \).

Net Capital Flow

This shows whether more capital is entering the protocol (subscriptions) than leaving (redemptions), in token units, over a chosen time period.

Formula 3 — Net Capital Flow
$$\text{Net Flow} = \sum \text{Subscriptions} - \sum \text{Redemptions}$$

A positive net flow means the protocol is growing. A negative net flow means more capital is leaving than entering — an early warning signal for the operations team.

Redemption Pressure

This metric answers the question: of all the transactions in a given period, what fraction are redemptions? High redemption pressure means the operations team should check whether the MSL pool has enough USDC to cover instant redemptions.

Formula 4 — Redemption Pressure
$$\text{Redemption Pressure} = \frac{\text{Number of Redemptions}}{\text{Number of Subscriptions} + \text{Number of Redemptions}} \times 100\%$$

The dashboard colours this indicator green (<25%), amber (25–50%), or red (>50%).

Average Deal Size

This tells the operations team whether activity is being driven by large institutional investors or many smaller transactions.

Formula 5 — Average Deal Size
$$\text{Avg Subscription Size} = \frac{\text{Total Subscribed Volume}}{\text{Number of Subscriptions}}$$

7-Day Supply Change

Tracks how much the total token supply has changed over the last 7 days. Calculated from the blockchain event history by summing all mint and burn events within the 7-day window.

Formula 6 — Supply Change (7 days)
$$\Delta\text{Supply}_{7d} = \text{Supply}_{\text{today}} - \text{Supply}_{7\text{d ago}}$$ $$\Delta\text{Supply}_{7d}\% = \frac{\Delta\text{Supply}_{7d}}{\text{Supply}_{7\text{d ago}}} \times 100\%$$
Section 6

The Five Dashboard Tabs

Operations
The morning health check. Shows oracle status, AUM, implied APY, 24h net flow, 7d supply trend, and the 5 most recent subscription/redemption events. Everything an ops associate needs in the first 60 seconds of their day.
Capital Flows
Detailed subscription and redemption management. Shows aggregates (total subscribed, total redeemed, net flow, redemption pressure) plus a daily bar chart and the full transaction table with USD values and counterparty addresses.
Access Control
Shows who is authorised to perform which actions on each contract. Roles are: Admin (controls the contract), Minter (processes subscriptions), Burner (processes redemptions), Depositor. Also shows the event log of all role grants and revocations.
Protocol Distribution
Shows where mTokens are deployed — how much is sitting in investor wallets vs deposited into DeFi protocols (e.g. Morpho). Includes a holder concentration analysis to assess single-redemption liquidity risk.
Runbook
Interactive workflow guide for all standard operations: subscription processing, redemption, whitelist updates, daily NAV update, product launch, and incident response. Each workflow has expandable step-by-step details and an interactive checklist to mark off as you go. Also contains a regulatory quick reference (MiCA, FMA, eligible jurisdictions).
Section 7

Where the Data Comes From

The dashboard fetches data from two distinct sources: the Etherscan API (for event history and transaction logs) and a direct RPC node connection (for reading live contract state). These are combined in the browser — no backend server is involved.

Metric shown Data source How it arrives
NAV price (current) Oracle contract on Ethereum RPC call: latestRoundData()
NAV price history (chart) Oracle contract event log Etherscan API: getLogs for AnswerUpdated events
Total supply mToken ERC-20 contract RPC call: totalSupply()
Subscriptions & Redemptions mToken Transfer event log Etherscan API: mint = Transfer from zero address; burn = Transfer to zero address
Role holders (Access Control) Contract RoleGranted / RoleRevoked events Etherscan getLogs + RPC hasRole() to confirm current state
Protocol balances (Distribution) mToken ERC-20 contract RPC call: balanceOf(protocolAddress) for each known protocol
Transaction calldata (Decoder) Etherscan transaction API Fetches raw input data then decodes against known function selectors
Why Etherscan V2?

The original Etherscan V1 API (api.etherscan.io/api) was deprecated. The dashboard was updated to use the V2 endpoint (api.etherscan.io/v2/api?chainid=1) which requires specifying the chain explicitly and uses an updated authentication model. This was a practical fix encountered during development.

Section 8

What to Explain in an Interview

This section maps every feature of the dashboard to what you already know from your work experience and studies — so you can speak naturally, not technically.

NAV pricing and the oracle

"The oracle is the on-chain equivalent of a Bloomberg price feed. Just as a Bloomberg terminal publishes the T-bill price at market close, the oracle publishes the fund NAV on Ethereum at the end of each trading day. I built the dashboard to check if that price is stale — same as you'd flag a data feed issue in a risk system."

Subscription and redemption workflow

"The lifecycle is identical to subscribing to a traditional fund — KYC check, USDC received, NAV strike, tokens allocated. The instant mode is analogous to a money market fund's same-day settlement. The standard mode maps to T+2/T+7 which I'm familiar with from the bond sales and agency lending desks at DekaBank."

MPC workspace and the 2-of-N quorum

"MPC (multi-party computation) is the institutional-grade custody model used instead of a hardware wallet. It's analogous to dual control in traditional finance — no single person can sign a payment unilaterally. At Midas, the strategy manager proposes and Midas co-signs. This is directly comparable to the co-signing requirements I encountered working on the smart contract framework for the Qivalis stablecoin consortium at DekaBank."

Redemption pressure and liquidity management

"Redemption pressure is essentially a real-time liquidity stress indicator. If redemptions start to outpace subscriptions — especially if large single redemptions exceed the MSL pool — that's the equivalent of a liquidity coverage ratio breach. I applied similar logic using the CAMELS framework during my regulatory analysis work at the State Bank of Pakistan."

Regulatory framework on the Runbook

"The product is structured as a tokenized certificate under German law, issued via a Luxembourg SPV, with an FMA-approved prospectus in Liechtenstein. Non-US, non-UK eligibility restrictions apply. MiCA reporting obligations are relevant where the product meets the asset-referenced token threshold. I included these in the runbook because they directly affect how you onboard new strategy managers or products — and it aligns with the MiCA and EBA regulatory skills on my CV."

The one-line summary

This dashboard bridges the gap between blockchain data (which lives as raw events on Ethereum) and institutional operations workflows (which require clean, actionable information). It was built to demonstrate that you can think about crypto infrastructure the same way you think about a Bloomberg terminal — as a data tool that serves a financial operations function.
